Ah, traditions and festivals, those worn-out crutches we lean on when the world seems to be falling apart. In times of political chaos and heart-wrenching events, these age-old customs take center stage, offering a brief distraction from the relentless march of reality.


It's intriguing how people flock to these tired ceremonies and celebrations when the ground beneath their feet feels shaky. Whether it's Oktoberfest in Germany, Thanksgiving in America, or Holi in India, these festivities serve as temporary escapism from the harsh truths of the present. They act as a band-aid over the gaping wounds of society, a feeble attempt to pretend that everything is fine for just a moment.


In the shadow of political unrest and tragedy, these traditions morph into more than just empty rituals—they become feeble gestures, attempts by communities to hold onto a semblance of normalcy in the face of chaos. They're like a child's security blanket in a hurricane, offering false comfort in a world gone mad.


And what about the supposed power of these celebrations to unite people? In a world where division reigns supreme, festivals and traditions often feel more like obligatory social obligations than genuine moments of connection. They're a temporary ceasefire in an ongoing war, a forced camaraderie that evaporates as soon as the music stops and the fireworks fade.


So, let's not get too carried away with the supposed significance of our cultural traditions and festivals. They may provide a fleeting sense of comfort and connection, but beneath the surface, they're fragile facades in a crumbling world. So, instead of romanticizing these tired rituals, let's acknowledge them for what they are—fleeting distractions in a world desperate for meaning and connection. Cheers to that, I suppose.
